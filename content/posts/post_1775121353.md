The Supreme Court heard arguments on Wednesday morning in the case of Trump v. Barbara, which challenges President Donald Trump's 2025 executive order banning birthright citizenship. While justices seemed skeptical of the administration's argument, their decision to take up the issue at all highlights how much ground nativists have gained since Trump's first term.

The 14th Amendment to the US Constitution guarantees citizenship to anyone born in the United States, regardless of their parents' immigration status. Trump's executive order sought to limit this provision, arguing that it incentivizes illegal immigration and undermines national security. However, legal scholars and immigrant rights advocates have long argued that birthright citizenship is a fundamental right, essential to the principles of equality and fairness.

During the oral arguments, justices pressed the administration on several key points, including the lack of evidence linking birthright citizenship to increased illegal immigration. They also questioned whether the president's order was legally sound, given the amendment's clear language. While it remains unclear how the Court will ultimately rule, the fact that they are considering the issue at all suggests that the nativist movement has made significant gains in recent years.

The outcome of this case will have far-reaching implications for immigrant communities and the broader US political landscape. If the administration's ban is upheld, it could lead to widespread legal uncertainty and potential harm to millions of people born on US soil. On the other hand, if the Court strikes down the order, it would be a major victory for advocates of immigration reform and a reaffirmation of the fundamental principles of American citizenship.
