As NASA gears up to launch its Artemis II mission, the space agency's ambitious plans for a lunar base are being called into question. The mission, which will see four astronauts travel around the Moon and back to Earth in 10 days' time, is meant to test out the hardware and systems that will eventually support human settlements on the lunar surface.

However, experts are pointing out that NASA's Artemis program may be legally dubious. Critics argue that the agency has not adequately consulted with international partners or obtained necessary permits for its plans to establish a permanent human presence on the Moon. The Outer Space Treaty, which was signed by over 100 countries in 1967, prohibits nations from claiming sovereignty over celestial bodies like the Moon.

Moreover, NASA's plans for a lunar base have raised concerns about environmental impact and the potential for contamination of the Moon's surface. The agency has not provided sufficient details on how it intends to mitigate these risks or address the long-term effects of establishing a human settlement on the Moon.

As the Artemis program continues to move forward, experts are calling for greater transparency and cooperation from NASA and other space-faring nations. With the prospect of commercial lunar missions and private companies like SpaceX and Blue Origin also eyeing the Moon, it remains to be seen whether NASA's plans will ultimately come to fruition or face significant legal and regulatory hurdles along the way.
