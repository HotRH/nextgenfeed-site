The Supreme Court heard arguments on Wednesday morning in Trump v. Barbara, a case that could determine the fate of President Donald Trump's 2025 executive order banning birthright citizenship. While justices appeared skeptical of the administration's argument, their decision to take up the issue at all serves as a stark reminder of how far nativist sentiments have advanced since Trump's first term.

The 14th Amendment to the US Constitution has long been interpreted as granting citizenship to anyone born within American borders, regardless of their parents' immigration status. However, Trump's executive order sought to reframe this understanding, claiming that birthright citizenship is not enshrined in the amendment and therefore can be revoked by presidential fiat.

As justices weighed the merits of the administration's argument, it became clear that many were unconvinced by the White House's legal reasoning. The Court's skeptical tone was evident as they peppered government lawyers with tough questions about the order's constitutional validity. Whether this skepticism translates into a ruling against the ban remains to be seen.

Regardless of the outcome, however, the fact that the Court chose to take up birthright citizenship at all is a significant development. By doing so, they have given nativist sentiments – which have long been on the rise in American politics – a prominent platform from which to advocate for further immigration restrictions and anti-foreigner sentiment.

The Trump administration's decision to challenge birthright citizenship was always intended as a symbolic move, designed to appeal to conservative base voters and further galvanize its hardline stance on immigration. And while this move may ultimately prove unsuccessful in the courts, it has already achieved its desired effect: normalizing nativist rhetoric and paving the way for future attempts to restrict legal entry into the United States.

In the end, the outcome of Trump v. Barbara will have significant implications for the thousands of individuals born on US soil who are currently facing uncertainty about their own citizenship status. But more broadly, this case serves as a harbinger of the intense battles that lie ahead in the realm of immigration policy – and the role that nativist sentiment is likely to play in shaping these debates for years to come.
