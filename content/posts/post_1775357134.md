The right-to-repair movement, which has gained significant momentum in the United States, faces a new challenge with the introduction of Colorado state bill SB26-090. This legislation seeks to exempt critical infrastructure from the state's consumer right-to-repair laws, a move that could significantly curtail the ability of individuals to fix and upgrade their own devices.

The passage of this bill would mark a significant shift in the direction of Colorado's right-to-repair efforts, which have been hailed as some of the most comprehensive in the country. In 2022, Colorado became the first state to pass legislation giving users the tools, instructions, and legal capabilities to fix or upgrade their own wheelchairs, agricultural farming equipment, and consumer electronics. Since then, similar efforts have rippled out across the country, with eight states passing repair bills.

However, tech manufacturers such as Cisco and IBM are strongly backing SB26-090, citing concerns over cybersecurity and intellectual property protection. According to lobbying disclosures, these companies stand to profit from controlling who fixes their products and what tools, components, and software are used in those repairs. This is a common theme among manufacturers opposed to right-to-repair laws, as they argue that giving individuals access to the necessary tools and information could also enable nefarious actors to use those methods for malicious purposes.
