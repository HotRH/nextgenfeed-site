NASA's Artemis II mission, set to launch on Wednesday, has sparked controversy surrounding the legality of the agency's plans for a lunar base. The historic mission will see four astronauts aboard the Orion capsule travel around the Moon before returning to Earth in 10 days' time, testing out the hardware and systems that will eventually support human settlements on the lunar surface.

However, legal experts are raising concerns over NASA's claim that it has exclusive rights to the Moon, citing a 1967 Outer Space Treaty that prohibits countries from claiming ownership or sovereignty over celestial bodies. According to the treaty, space is to be used for peaceful purposes and the exploration of space is open to all nations.

Critics argue that NASA's plans to establish a permanent human settlement on the Moon could undermine the international treaty, potentially setting a harmful precedent for future space exploration and exploitation. The controversy has sparked heated debate within the space community, with some advocating for greater transparency and cooperation in the development of lunar infrastructure.

The Artemis II mission marks a significant step forward in NASA's plans to return humans to the Moon by 2024 and establish a sustainable presence on the lunar surface. As tensions surrounding the project continue to escalate, it remains to be seen whether the agency can overcome legal and ethical concerns to achieve its ambitious goals.
