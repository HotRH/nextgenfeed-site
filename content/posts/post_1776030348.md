As technology enthusiasts await Apple's foray into the world of smart glasses, rumors are swirling about the company's plans to unveil its first pair in 2027. According to Bloomberg's reliable sources, Apple is testing four distinct designs for its upcoming smart glasses, which could potentially debut as soon as this year. While the concept may not be revolutionary, a closer examination of these designs and features reveals a more nuanced approach from Apple, one that diverges from its earlier ambitions in mixed and augmented reality.

The four design options reportedly under consideration by Apple include a large rectangular frame, a slimmer rectangular frame akin to those worn by CEO Tim Cook, a larger oval or circular frame, and a smaller oval or circular frame. Additionally, the company is exploring various color options, such as black, ocean blue, and light brown. While these designs may not stray far from traditional glasses, they do suggest a focus on functionality over flashy innovation.

In contrast to Apple's earlier plans for mixed and augmented reality devices, which were met with delays and lukewarm reception, the company's smart glasses appear more modest in their aspirations. These devices won't feature displays, but instead will rely on voice commands, allowing users to take photos and videos, answer phone calls, play music, and interact with Siri, the long-promised AI upgrade. This approach bears a resemblance to Meta's Ray-Ban smart glasses, which eschew display screens in favor of subtle, functional design.

The decision to focus on camera lenses instead of displays may be a deliberate choice by Apple to differentiate its product from other smart glasses on the market. By emphasizing photography and video capabilities, Apple is tapping into the growing trend of visual storytelling, where users are increasingly reliant on their mobile devices to capture and share life's moments. In this sense, the company's smart glasses can be seen as a natural evolution of its iPhone and camera business.

Ultimately, whether Apple's smart glasses succeed or fail will depend on how well they balance form and function. If the company can strike the right chord by creating a device that is both stylish and practical, then it may have found a niche in the competitive wearables market. However, if the product falls short of expectations, Apple may be forced to reevaluate its strategy once again, as it has done numerous times throughout its history.
