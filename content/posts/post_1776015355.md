Artificial intelligence (AI) is a vast and intricate field, with researchers and scientists relying on technical jargon and terminology to describe their work. As we delve deeper into the world of AI, it's essential to understand the fundamental concepts that underpin this technology. In this article, we'll explore key terms that are crucial to grasping the essence of AI, from artificial general intelligence (AGI) to deep learning.

Artificial General Intelligence (AGI)

AGI is a term shrouded in mystery, and even experts in the field struggle to define it precisely. Simply put, AGI refers to AI systems that are capable of performing tasks that surpass human capabilities at many or most economically valuable activities. The definition can vary depending on the source; for instance, OpenAI's CEO Sam Altman described AGI as "the equivalent of a median human that you could hire as a co-worker." Meanwhile, Google DeepMind views AGI as AI that is at least as capable as humans at most cognitive tasks. The ambiguity surrounding AGI underscores the complexity and nuance of this technology.

AI Agents

An AI agent is an autonomous system that uses AI technologies to perform a series of tasks on behalf of its users. This can include filing expenses, booking tickets, or even writing and maintaining code. However, the concept of an AI agent remains emergent, with infrastructure still being built out to deliver on its envisioned capabilities. At its core, an AI agent is an autonomous system that may draw on multiple AI systems to carry out multistep tasks.

Chain of Thought

In a human brain, chain-of-thought reasoning allows for quick and effortless answers to simple questions, such as "which animal is taller, a giraffe or a cat?" However, when faced with more complex problems, humans often require pen and paper to arrive at the correct solution. In an AI context, chain-of-thought reasoning for large language models involves breaking down a problem into smaller, intermediate steps to improve the quality of the end result. This approach typically takes longer but yields more accurate results, especially in logic or coding contexts.

Compute

Compute refers to the vital computational power that enables AI models to operate. This processing power fuels the AI industry, allowing for the training and deployment of powerful models. Compute is often shorthand for the hardware infrastructure that provides this power, including GPUs, CPUs, TPUs, and other forms of computing infrastructure that underpin the modern AI industry.

Deep Learning

Deep learning is a subset of self-improving machine learning that involves designing AI algorithms with a multi-layered, artificial neural network (ANN) structure. This allows for more complex correlations compared to simpler machine learning-based systems. The structure draws inspiration from the interconnected pathways of neurons in the human brain. Deep learning AI models can identify important characteristics in data themselves, rather than relying on human engineers to define these features. However, deep learning systems require a vast amount of data points (millions or more) and typically take longer to train compared to simpler machine learning algorithms, resulting in higher development costs.

As we continue to explore the complex world of AI, understanding these fundamental terms is crucial for grasping the intricacies of this technology. By delving deeper into the concepts that underpin AI, we can better appreciate its potential and limitations, ultimately driving innovation and progress in this rapidly evolving field.
