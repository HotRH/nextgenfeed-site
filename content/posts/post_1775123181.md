With NASA planning to launch four astronauts on Wednesday on its Artemis II mission, the race to return to the Moon is back on. The current mission will see astronauts aboard the Orion capsule travel around the Moon before returning to Earth in 10 days' time. They'll be testing out the hardware and systems that will eventually support a lunar base, but critics are questioning the legal foundations of this ambitious project.

While NASA touts its Artemis program as a historic moment for space exploration, some experts argue that the agency's plans for establishing a permanent human settlement on the Moon may be legally dubious. The Outer Space Treaty, which was signed by over 100 countries including the United States in 1967, prohibits governments from claiming ownership or sovereignty over celestial bodies like the Moon.

Despite this, NASA is moving forward with its plan to establish a lunar base as part of its Artemis program. The agency has identified several potential locations on the Moon's surface for the base, which it hopes will serve as a stepping stone for further human exploration and potentially even settlement of deep space. However, critics argue that NASA's plans may be at odds with the Outer Space Treaty's provisions.

As the debate continues, NASA is set to launch its Artemis II mission on Wednesday, marking another major milestone in its quest to return humans to the Moon. While the mission's primary goal is to test out the hardware and systems necessary for a lunar base, it also raises questions about the legal framework that will govern such an endeavor.
