The United States space agency NASA is set to launch its Artemis II mission on Wednesday, marking a major milestone in the push to return humans to the Moon. The four-astronaut crew will embark on a journey around the lunar body before returning to Earth after 10 days, with the primary objective of testing the Orion spacecraft and its supporting systems. While this endeavor may seem like a significant step forward for space exploration, some experts are raising concerns about the legal underpinnings of NASA's ambitious plan – namely, the construction of a permanent human settlement on the Moon's surface. Specifically, they argue that NASA's actions may be violating international law, which prohibits the exploitation or colonization of celestial bodies without prior agreements and cooperation with other nations. As the space agency continues to push forward with its plans for a lunar base, these legal questions are likely to remain at the forefront of the debate.
